The list of handheld game consoles documents notable handheld game consoles released as commercial products. Handheld game consoles are portable video game consoles with a built-in screen and game controls and the ability to play multiple and separate video games. It does not include PDAs, smartphones, or tablet computers; while those devices are often capable of playing games, they are not generally classified as video game consoles. This is not a complete list; it only lists handheld game consoles with its own Wikipedia article and a source verifying its classification as a handheld console. Currently there are 57 entries in this list, 4 consoles were canceled.[note 1]

  • First of the Game Boy line of handhelds.[1]
  • Plays monochrome games from ROM cartridges.[1]
  • Hardware revisions include the smaller Game Boy Pocket in 1996, and color screened Game Boy Color in 1998.[10][1]
  • 1,244 games released.[11]
  • Was the best-selling handheld console until 2010 when it was surpassed by the Nintendo DS.[12]

Atari Lynx (Atari)
  • First handheld electronic game with a color LCD,[3] 3.5-inch screen.[1]
  • Plays ROM cartridges[14]
  • Hardware revision smaller Atari Lynx II released in 1991.[3]
  • Less than 100 games released.[14]
  • Considered a commercial failure.[3]

Game Gear (Sega)
  • Sega's first handheld game console, color screen with backlight.[16]
  • Plays ROM cartridges[17]
  • Internally similar to the Master System, could play games with a converter add-on.[17]
  • Technology more advanced than its competitors, but suffered from short battery life.[16]
  • Not a commercial failure; outsold many competitors, but sales lagged far behind Game Boy.[16][3][18]
 1990[19] 11,000,000[16] [1]
TurboExpress (NEC)
  • Internally similar to NEC's TurboGrafx-16 home console, directly able to play its games.[3]
  • Plays ROM cartridges[1]
  • Technologically advanced for its time, but high price and short battery life plagued its commercial appeal.[1][3]
 1990[20] 1,500,000[15] [1]
Gamate (Bit Corporation)
  • Technologically similar to the original Game Boy.[21]
  • Plays monochrome games on large, credit card sized ROM cartridges.[21]
  • Hardware revisions were reported, but they were largely cosmetic and trivial.[21]
  • At least 71 games are known to have been released.[21]
  • No exact sales figures are known, but it is generally considered to be a commercial failure.[21]
 1990[21] [21]
Game Master (Hartung)
  • Has a similar physical appearance to a Game Gear.[18]
  • Technologically far behind the Game Boy, despite Game Boy generally being considered the weakest at the time frame.[18]
  • Plays monochrome games from ROM cartridges[18]
  • Considered a commercial failure.[18]
 1990[18] [18]
Watara Supervision
  • Technologically similar to the Game Boy.[18]
  • Screen could be tilted relative to controls via flexible connection.[22]
  • Hardware revision/version that looked very similar to the Game Boy.[18]
  • Plays monochrome games from ROM cartridges.[22]
  • Could link to a television via a link cable.[22]
  • Considered a commercial failure, often cited due to a lack of games.[22][18]
 1992[18] [18]
Mega Duck (Welback Holdings)
  • Technologically and physically similar to the Game Boy.[18]
  • Plays monochrome games from ROM cartridges.[18]
  • Considered a commercial failure, often cited due to lack of games.[18][22]
 1993[18] [18]
Nomad (Sega)
  • Sega's second and last traditional handheld.
  • Played entire Sega Genesis library and its exact ROM cartridges.[1]
  • No games specifically made for it, no compatibility for any other Sega platforms.[3]
  • Suffered from fast battery consumption and launching at a time when Sega trying to support many other platforms concurrently.[18][3]
  • Considered a commercial failure.[18][1]

Game.com (Tiger Electronics)
  • First handheld to feature a touchscreen and internet connection.[3]

  • Hardware revision Game.com Pocket Pro released in 1998.[3]
  • Considered a commercial failure.[15][3]
 1997[3] 300,000[15] [3]

Neo Geo Pocket (SNK)
  • Part of the Neo Geo family of consoles.[24]
  • Plays monochrome games from ROM cartridges.[24]
  • Hardware revision Neo Geo Pocket Color replaces in 1999 due to poor sales.[15][24]
  • Considered a commercial failure, and SNK subsequently leaves the hardware business.[15][24]
 1998[25] 2,000,000[15] [1]
WonderSwan (Bandai)
  • Created by Game Boy creator Gunpei Yokoi after leaving Nintendo.[3]
  • Plays monochrome games from ROM cartridges[3]
  • Hardware revisions of WonderSwan Color released in 2000 and SwanCrystal in 2002.[26]
  • Considered a commercial failure, especially after the 2001 release of the Game Boy Advance.[1]
 1999[1] 3,500,000[27][28] [1]
Cybiko
  • Combination PDA and handheld game console.[29]
  • Plays digital games via internet download from PC.[29]
  • More than 430 games and applications produced, all free.[30]
  • Hardware revision Cybiko Xtreme released in September 2001.[29]
 2000[29] 500,000[31] [32]
Game Boy Advance (Nintendo)
  • Part of the Game Boy line of handheld consoles.[1]
  • Backwards compatible with Game Boy and Game Boy Color games.[33]
  • Plays games from ROM cartridges.[33]
  • Hardware revision Game Boy Advance SP released in 2003, smaller Game Boy Micro in 2005.[3]
  • Roughly 1,500 games released.[33]
  • Considered a commercial success despite its shorter lifespan due to the release of the Nintendo DS in 2004.[3][1]
 2001[34] 81,500,000[35] [1]
GP32 (Game Park)
  • Open source hardware handheld released in South Korea.[1]
  • Plays SmartMedia cards and digital games via internet download.[3]
  • Most releases for the system were open-source software games and applications.[3]
  • Few commercial games released, as publishers feared its openness would lead to piracy.[3]
  • Considered a commercial failure, but credited as influential in the world of open source hardware.[1]
 2001[3] 32,000[36] [1]
P/ECE (AQUAPLUS)
  • Open source hardware handheld released only in Japan.[37]
  • Plays digital games downloaded via connecting to a computer with USB-B.[38]
  • Most releases for the system were open-source software games and applications.[38]
  • Could interact with select computer games while connected to a computer, mostly visual novels and eroge.[39]
 2001[37]
N-Gage (Nokia)
  • Combination handheld game console and phone.[3][40]
  • Plays MultiMediaCards
  • Hardware revision N-Gage QD released in 2004.[1]
  • Considered a critical and commercial failure.[3][1]
 2003[41][3] 3,000,000[15] [1]
GameKing
  • Series of low end handhelds
 2003 [42]
Tapwave Zodiac
  • Combination handheld game console and PDA.[1][3]
  • Features a touchscreen with an included stylus and an MP3 player.[3]
  • Plays digital games via internal memory or SD cards.[3]
  • Received critical acclaim for its concept, but was a commercial failure, especially after Sony's 2004 release of the PlayStation Portable.[1]
 2003[1] 200,000[15] [1]
Nintendo DS (Nintendo)
  • Successor to the Game Boy Advance line, first console in the Nintendo DS line.[43]
  • Feature two separate screens, one of which a touch screen with a stylus.[43]
  • Hardware revisions include Nintendo DS Lite in 2006, Nintendo DSi in 2008, Nintendo DSi XL in 2009.[43]
  • All models play DS ROM cartridges; regular DS models compatible with Game Boy Advance games, DSi models dropped GBA support but added limited library of digital download games.[33]
  • Considered a commercial success; best-selling handheld of all time, and second best-selling console of all time behind the PlayStation 2.[44]
 2004[1] 154,020,000[45] [1]
PlayStation Portable (Sony)
  • Sony's first traditional entry into the handheld console market.[3]
  • Plays proprietary Universal Media Discs and digital download games via internet.[46]
  • Minor hardware revisions include PSP-2000 in 2007, PSP-3000 in 2008.[3]
  • Major hardware revisions include UMD drive-less PSP Go in 2009, wifi-connection-less PSP E1000 in 2011.[3]
  • Its legacy is mixed; its sales are far more than any other non-Nintendo handheld, but its sales are just over half of its main competitor of the time, the Nintendo DS.[3]
 2004 80,000,000[47] [1]
Gizmondo (Tiger Telematics)
  • Plays SD cards.[48]
  • Features camera, GPS, text messaging, and Bluetooth wireless connectivity.[49]
  • Despite features, technologically well behind main competitors of the time of PlayStation Portable and Nintendo DS.[48]
  • Had an alternative "Smart Ads" model released concurrently which sold at almost half the price, but required daily streaming advertisements to be watched on the handheld, well before smartphone games popularized the concept.[49]
  • Considered a severe commercial failure, propelled by a juxtaposition low sales and particularly high promotional spending and investments.[49][1]
 2005[1] 25,000[15] [1]
GP2X (GamePark Holdings)
  • Open source hardware handheld released only in South Korea and the UK.[36]
  • Technologically and physically similar to the PlayStation Portable.[36]
  • Plays digital games via SD cards and internet download.[36]
  • In addition to games, features movie, e-book, and photo viewing.[36]
  • Most releases for the system were open-source software games and applications.[36]
 2005[50] >60,000[51] [52]
Dingoo A320 (Dingo Digital Technology)
  • Open source hardware handheld game console with music and video playback functions.[53]
  • Plays digital distribution games via internet download and mini-SD cards.[53]
  • Sold with connection accessories to playing on a television.[53]
  • Limited retail software.[53]
 2009[53] [53]
GP2X Wiz (GamePark Holdings)
  • Open source hardware handheld game console.[54]
  • Successor to the GP2X.[54]
  • Possesses upgrades in screen and button quality, but only minor upgrade in processing power.[54]
  • Plays digital games via internet download, few retail games.[54]
 2009[54] [54]
Pandora (OpenPandora)
  • Open source hardware handheld game console.[55]
  • Combination game console and subnotebook; appearance of a laptop shrunk down to the size of a Nintendo 3DS.[56]
  • Plays digital games via internet download.[56]
  • Features a touchscreen, full QWERTY keyboard, and TV-out.[56]
 2010[55] [55]
CAANOO (GamePark Holdings)
  • Open source hardware handheld game console.[49]
  • Successor to the GP2X Wiz[57]
  • Plays digital games via internet download.[57]
  • Considered a commercial failure.[49]
 2010[49] [49]
Nintendo 3DS (Nintendo)
  • Successor to the Nintendo DS line, start of the Nintendo 3DS line.[58]
  • Hardware revisions include the Nintendo 3DS XL, Nintendo 2DS and New Nintendo 3DS.[58]
  • Uses two separate screens and is capable of projecting stereoscopic 3D effects without use of 3D glasses.[58]
  • Plays cartridges and digital games via internet download.[59]
  • Mixed legacy; a commercial success as far as 75 million sold put it among the best selling handhelds, and well above main competitor PlayStation Vita, but a large sales drop from predecessor Nintendo DS, and quickly dropped after its own successor, Nintendo Switch, was released.[58]
 2011[60] 75,940,000[61] [1]
PlayStation Vita (Sony)
  • Sony's second handheld console, successor to the PlayStation Portable.[62]
  • Two models launched; a regular one, and one with 3G internet capabilities that was quickly phased out.[62]
  • Minor hardware revisions model "PCH-2000" released in 2013
  • Mixed legacy; was a commercial failure, but retained a cult following, and is cited as influential in the successful PlayStation 4 home console released after it.[62]
 2011[62] 16,000,000[62] [62]
Neo Geo X (Tommo)
  • Part of the Neo Geo line, releases are adaptations of past Neo Geo titles.[63]
  • Plays games loaded on SD cards, no digital distribution.[63]
  • Contains HDMI, A/V and control docking ports for connecting to a television.[63]
  • The publishing and distribution was licensed to Tommo from SNK Playmore.[63]
  • Considered a commercial failure for both parties; SNK ordered a halt on production after consumer complaints on quality, which in turn hurt Tommo financially. Both parties threatened legal action.[64]
 2012[65] [64]
Game Gadget
  • Plays a number of licensed Sega games
  • Linux based handheld
 2012 ~20,000[66]
GCW Zero (Game Consoles Worldwide)
  • Linux-based on open source hardware.[67]
  • Focus on homebrew and emulation.[68]
  • Relatively small-scale, niche production funded by Kickstarter.[67][68]
 2013[68] [68]
Nvidia Shield Portable
  • Has a physical appearance of an Xbox 360 controller with a 5-inch screen grafted on top of it.[69]
  • Based on Android operating system.[70]
  • Supports HDMI connection to television or wireless game streaming from PC.[70]
 2012[71] [69]
GPD XD (GamePad Digital)
  • Android-based[72]
  • Plays game downloaded through the Google Play store[72]
  • Contains joystick and touchscreen[72]
 2015 [72]
Arduboy
  • Open source hardware based on the Arduino hardware platform
  • The original version was 1.6 mm thick, with the height and width of a credit card
  • Games published on Arduboy Arcade are free, open source and available to be edited
  • 'Arduboy FX', an upgraded version, includes a flash memory chip that stores over 250 games on the device itself
 2016 [73]
GPD Win (GamePad Digital)
  • Microsoft Windows-based handheld and PC hybrid.[74]
  • Has full QWERTY keyboard, joysticks, d-pad, and face-buttons.[75]
  • Increasingly more powerful hardware iterations called GPD Win 2, GPD Win Max, GPD Win 3, GPD Win 4 and GPD Win Mini.[75]
 2016[75] [75]
Nintendo Switch (Nintendo)
  • Can be played as a handheld or on a television when put into docking station.[1]
  • The individual Joy-Con controllers can attach to the main unit or be used separately.[76]
  • Touchscreen display with 720p resolution, up to 1080p when docked via HDMI port.[77]
  • Hardware revision in the handheld-only Nintendo Switch Lite in 2019, larger OLED screen model in 2021.[78]
  • Considered a commercial success; passing 125 million sold by 2023.[79]
 2017 154,010,000[80]
Evercade (Blaze Entertainment)
  • Plays officially licensed collections of emulated retro video games.[81]
  • Design similar to original Game Boy Advance, has ability to connect to television with additional HDMI accessory.[82]
  • Games released through curated collections of games compiled onto a game cartridge.[81]
  • Supported by companies such as Atari, Namco, and Interplay.[81][82]
 2020[83] [81]
Analogue Pocket (Analogue)
  • Plays original game cartridge for Game Boy, Game Boy Color, Game Boy Advance, Game Gear, Neo Geo Pocket, Neo Geo Pocket Color and Atari Lynx games.
  • Has a 3.5" 1600x1440, 615 ppi Display.
  • Designed with Analogue OS, an database based operating system
  • Features Save States with original game cartridges.
 2021 [84]
Ayaneo
  • Handheld Windows gaming PC using AMD Ryzen processors
  • Various models include Aya Neo (2021), Ayaneo Next (2022) and Ayaneo Air/Ayaneo Air Pro (2022).
 2021 [85][86][87]
Steam Deck (Valve Corporation)
  • A handheld console that plays most games in the Steam library.[88]
  • Ability to run games from other game stores as well - (Epic Games Store, uPlay, etc.)[88]
  • Comes in 3 models; one LCD and two OLED models, all with different storage capacities.[89]
  • Has 7 inch, 800p touchscreen.[88]
  • Ability to hook up to PC monitor or television.[88]
 2022[90] ≈3,000,000 as of 2023[91] [92][93]
Thumby (TinyCircuits)
  • Very small formfactor handheld measuring 1.2 by 0.7 by 0.3 inches (30.5 mm × 17.8 mm × 7.6 mm).
  • Has a 0.38 by 0.27 inches (9.7 mm × 6.9 mm) black and white OLED display.
  • Can be mounted on a keychain.
  • Can play multiplayer games with a link cable.
 2022 [94]
Playdate (Panic)

  • Has a 2.7-inch black and white display.[95]
  • New games released once per week.[96]
  • Initial purchase of unit give user access to the first "season" of games (24 total.)[96]
 2022 [95]
